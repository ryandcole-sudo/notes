Memoir on the speed of light

from the first class at the Institute, 10 December 1810
by M. Arago

The determination of the colossal speed with which light travels through space is, without doubt, one of the most beautiful results of modern astronomy. The ancients believed this speed to be infinite; and their perspective in this regard was not, as with so many other questions of physics, a mere opinion devoid of evidence; because Aristotle, in presenting it, cites in its support the instantaneous transmission of daylight. This opinion was later challenged by Alhazen,in his Book of Optics, but only through metaphysical reasoning, in which Porta, the commentator, who assumes what he calls the immateriality of light, also objected with very bad arguments. Galileo appears to have been the first, amongst the moderns, to seek to determine this speed through experiment. In the first dialogue of the Two New Sciences, Salviati, one of the three interlocutors, laid out a most ingenious experiment that he had done, and that he thought could resolve the question. Two observers, with two lights, having placed them close to a distance of a mile apart: one of them, at any instant turns off his light; the second covers his light immediately when he no longer sees the other; but since the first observer sees the second light disappear at the same time as he covers his light, Galileo concludes that light is transmitted, in a very small amount of time, a distance double the amount that separates the two observers. Some experiments similar to this conducted by the members of the Accademia del Cimento, but with three times as much distance, lead to the same result.

These experiments, at first glance, seem rather paltry, when we consider the grandeur of their objective, but we judge less harshly when we remember that around the same time, men such as Lord Bacon, whose merits are widely appreciated, believed that the speed of light, like that of sound, could be perceptibly altered by the strength and direction of the wind.

Descartes, whose the system of light has much in common with what we call the undulatory system, thought that light was transmitted instantaneously to all distances; he further bases this opinion on conclusions drawn from the observation of eclipses of the moon. One must admit that his rather ingenious argument proves, if not that the speed of light is infinite, at least that it is more considerable than all those whereby one could be flattered to determine by direct experiment made on Earth in the manner of Galileo.

The frequent eclipses of Jupiter's first satellite, closely following the discovery of telescopes, provided Römer with the first demonstration we have of the (successive) motion of light. The still imperfect knowledge we had of the motion of other satellites, the difficulty of observing their eclipses exactly, and some unknown inequalities which, by combining with what depended on the motion of light, masked their effects, made them less salient and consequently prevented its recognition and caused for some time the rejection of Römer's discovery; it was only generally accepted when Bradley had shown that this annual motion to which the stars were subjected, which is called aberration, depended on the combined effect of the motion of light and that of the observer. The speed which we had deduced from this phenomenon differed slightly from that which we obtained from the eclipses of the first satellite; but the perfection to which the Tables were brought, in the works of Laplace, has made it possible to revise the first calculations; the constant of aberration that M. Delambre has found by the (discussion) of a very large number of eclipses of satellites, is absolutely the same as that which Bradley had found through his observations.

The first consequence that can be drawn from this remarkable agreement is that light moves uniformly, or at least without any perceptible variations in the space comprising the orbit of the Earth; the eccentricity of the orbit of Jupiter makes it possible to extend this result to the immense range it encompasses. It is moreover quite natural to suppose that stars of various sizes are unequally distant; and their absolute aberrations, deduced from direct observation, are the same. Bradley had concluded that the motion of light is uniform at all distances, and that the aberration of all celestial bodies can be calculated with the same constant. Some astronomers, however, did not accept this result; they suspected that the stars of various sizes can emit rays with different speeds, and it must be admitted that this idea especially in the emission system, was at the same time natural and probable. The direct observation of aberration was hardly suited to resolve the question in a decisive manner, since differences in speed equal to about 1⁄ 20 of the speed of light, would produce in aberration only a difference of 1'', that one could not hope to surpass, even with the help of the best implements; but if we remember that the deviation experienced by the luminous rays, penetrating obliquely into a diaphanous bodies is a determined function of their incident velocity, we see that the total deviation to which they are subject when traversing a prism furnishes a natural measurement of their velocity. This method is capable of rendering slight inequalities perceptible, because, as it is easy to show, a difference of 1 ⁄ 20 produces in the deviations a difference of 2' even supposing that we don't utilize a prism with angle exceeding 45'. This is also the path I had followed in the experiment, the result of which I had the honor of communicating The Class, more than 4 years ago; luminous rays from various stars, the sun, moon, planets and terrestrial lights, had undergone the same deviation, The largest discrepancies amounting to 5'', and this number, which is the sum of the errors of observation and declination, corresponding moreover only to a 1⁄480 and to 1⁄24 of a second on the aberration; I had concluded from these results that light moves with the same speed, no matter the bodies from which it emanates, or at least if there are some differences, they cannot, in any way, alter the accuracy the accuracy of the astronomical observations.

Since reading my memoir M. Calendreli has published in the Astronomical Pamphlets, printed in Rome, some experiments done using this method, and which lead him to the same conclusion, except as it regards to sunlight, which he attributes to a particular refraction; but I am sure that this last result, of which we cannot admit accuracy, is that, when observing the stars the Roman astronomer aimed directly at the center of the yellow light. While for the sun, as he was forced to observe the edge, he pointed, on the contrary, to one of the extreme colors of the spectrum: moreover, it would be enough for me to justify, regardless of these considerations, the results I have reached, to notice that M. Calendreli finds, as well as myself, that the spots on the moon, which we see only through reflected sunlight are refracted by precisely the same amount as stars.

We see, moreover, that the certainty of the conclusion we make regarding the speed of light, from the observations made using prism, is based on the supposition that an inequality of speed produces an inequality in the deviation, which follows immediately from Newton's explanation of refraction; the experiments which I have cited, made me see the possibility of demonstrating this principle, but the work on the meridian arc had lead me to abandon this research; I have since resumed, subsequent to my return, the results of which I will communicate to the Class today.

My experiment was almost complete when the reading of one of the beautiful memoirs, which Dr. Young has inserted in the Philosophical Transactions, informed me that Mr. Robisson, professor of physics at Edinburgh, had considered this question of the speed of light theoretically; I have since found that it had been examined from different perspectives by Boscowich, Michell, Wilson and Blair.

Before speaking of my observations, I believe I should indicate that the experiments of the physicist just cited have not been published in this respect.

The idea of seeking to measure, by direct experiment, the increase in the speed of light rays passing from a rarer medium to a denser medium, must have naturally presented itself to a large number of persons; but Boscowich is, to my knowledge, is the first who published a reasonable experimental test of this idea. This physicist believed that by observing the stars through a telescope filled with water, he would find, due to an increase in speed acquired by the rays penetrating into this liquid, and aberration different from that observed when the space between the object and the eyepiece is filled with air. This same circumstance was to bring about perceptible changes in the position of terrestrial objects, which would thus have been subject to diurnal aberration. He found, for example, that a chart located to the south in the winter solstice, would have described, in twenty-four hours, a circle with a radius = 5'', and whose center corresponds to the average position of the object; but Boscowich's reasoning is defective because he forgot to take into account refraction,, and consequently of the change in direction that the rays must experience by penetrating obliquely from glass into liquid. Adding to that, Mr. Wilson, professor of astronomy at Glasgow, who published in the philosophical transactions, for the year 1782 a memoir, in which he also uses a telescope filled with water as a means of testing the Newtonian theory, but arrives at the complete opposite conclusions to those of Boscowich; for he has proven that aberration in a similar telescope will not be equal to that found in an ordinary instrument, only in the case where the velocities in the rare and diaphanous media are in the ratio assigned by Newton. Additionally, we can (remark) that the need to apply high magnifications to instruments which are intended to discover small quantities made Boscowich's telescope useless, since the light of star would be, if not totally extinguished, considerably weakened when passing through a depth of liquid 3 or 4 feet.

The difficulty which presents to us, in attempting to verify Newton's theory, results from this principle which is a consequence of it, namely: that the speed of light, in any diaphanous medium, must be the same, whatever the nature and number places it has previously passed through. It can however be observed that when refracting bodies are in motion, the refraction experienced by the rays should no longer be calculated with its absolute speed, but instead with this speed, increased or decreased by that of the body, that is to say the relative speed of the ray; the motion we can (imprint) to bodies on Earth, been far too small to meaningfully affect the refraction of light, it is necessary to seek in faster moving planets, circumstances better suited to render sensitive these refractive inequalities. Wilson, had proposed this method to measure the translational velocity of the Earth. Dr. Blair, to whom we are indebted for his very interesting work on the dispersive forces of liquids, believed that the observations had to render perceptible the inequality in speeds with which the light rays that reach us from the two edges of Jupiter are reflected, because of the rotation of the planet on its axis; and Mr. Robisson, in a certain memoir, where he examines this question of the speed of light in details, also mentions observations of two edges of the rings of Saturn.

Such were the means which these distinguished scholars had proposed to solve the problems which concerned progress in both physics and astronomy; it also follows from the historical summary just given, that they were also attached to tracing the path that should be followed to arrive at a decisive result, than to undertake observations of which they doubtless foresaw great difficulty. I thought it would be important to use the means offered by the current state of our knowledge and great precision of our instruments, in examining a question whose result may offer us a clue as to the true nature of light.

I have endeavored, in my experiment, to make perceptible the differences which must result from the translational motion of the Earth, because that of our system could, by combining with the former, give rise to rather large inequalities. It is, furthermore, natural to suppose that just as there are stars of various brightness in the sky, there are also some of various sizes, and this circumstance, which I believe was first shown by M. Michell, must occasion very-(perceptible) differences in speed of the rays that emanate from these various bodies; additionally, this kind of experiment allowed me to observe it with a telescope of short focus, while it would be essential to use strong magnification to observe inequalities of planetary diameters. This method would further require that prisms be very perfect, since defects of achromatic defects are proportional to magnification. I have already made some test, with the aid of M. Rochon's excellent prismatic micrometer, which gave me hope of succeeding; I will, while waiting on the results, communicate to the Class the result of the first method, which to me seem preferable in all respects.

When we look at an object through a prism, the inequalities of the deviation, which arise from changed in speed of the ray, must be all the more considerable as the angle of the prism will itself be larger; but when we use simple prisms or those formed from a single substance, there is, in this respect, a limit we cannot exceed, because, if the angle of the prism exceeds 4 or 5 degrees, the edges of the spectrum are diffuse; and as the passage from a prismatic color to the neighbouring one is made by an imperceptible degradation, one cannot have any certainty, in each observation, of pointing to parts of the corresponding spectra; achromatic prisms whose angle can be increase at will, are much better able to resolving the object I have in view.

The one I used for my first experiment was a prism made of crown-glass and one made of flint-glass; the difference in their angles, or the total prism angle, was approximately equal to 2.4 degrees. In order to reduce, as far as possible, the partial reflections that light always experiences at surface separating media of very different densities, I had my two prisms glued together with the resin used by opticians to remove polish defects on the inner surface of the lenses. The whole prism was [placed], in an (unshakable) manner, in a box whose side pins could turn in (collars), which made it possible to give the external face an inclination which made the image clearest. In order to be sure to observe in the plane of the refracting angle, a lateral movement had also been arranged, by a mechanism that would be too difficult to describe here; it would be enough for me to say that the whole apparatus would be fixed by strong screws to the outside cover of the telescope of (mural).
