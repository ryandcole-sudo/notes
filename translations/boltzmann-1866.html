On the Mechanical Interpretation of the Second Law of Thermodynamics

The identity of the first law of thermodynamics with the law of the living force has long been established; on the other hand, the second law occupies a peculiar and exceptional position, and its proofs are carried out here and there in ways that are in no way certain and by no means straightforward.

The purpose of this treatise then, is to provide of purely analytical, completely general proof of the second law of thermodynamics, as well as to determine the corresponding mechanical laws.

Definition of the Concept of Temperature

Firstly, we must define the central concept in all of thermodynamics, which so far has yet to be defined with any clarity and agreement, namely the concept of temperature. We would obviously proceed most naturally in the spirit of thermodynamics, if we were to first establish the experimental definition of temperature then determine which functions of the quantities that define molecular motion has such properties, so as to be regarded as representing that which is manifest to us in nature as temperature. But the first and most necessary requirement for defining temperature is that bodies of equal temperature, brought into contact with one another do not exchange heat, i.e no vis viva in atomic motion, and we have to find the conditions necessary for this equilibrium. For this purpose let us consider two bodies that are in contact, which consists of atoms with forces acting between them, the intensity of which is generally a function of the distance between two atoms. Additionally, the contact should be such that no compression or expansion of either body occurs, as that would always result in temperature changes and thereby disturb the equilibrium of heat; in other words, the pressure should be the same in all directions. Let us now specifically examine the interaction of an atom of the first body with mass \(m\) and speed \(c\), as well as an atom of the second body with mass \(M\) and speed \(C\). We assume the velocities of the other atoms are such that on average they neither absorb vis viva from the atoms \(m\) and \(M\), nor give it to them, nor do they alter the component of the overall motion of the common center of gravity of both atoms in any direction; indeed, this must be so if there is to be a stability of both bodies in our sense. (For the case where both bodies are at rest, the motion of the common center of gravity \(=0\) ; however, it is not necessary to introduce this restriction. ) Let us now examine the case where the atom \(m\) also does not receive vis viva from \(M\) on average; i.e, there is thermal equilibrium between them. It follows from our assumption that after a certain time, the start and end of which shall be denoted by \(t_1\) and \(t_2\), the sum of the vis viva of both atoms, as well as the motion of the common center of gravity in any direction, will again assume the same values; so that, if we therefore denote by \(v\) and \(V\) the velocities after this time, \(\phi\) and \(\Phi\) the angles of the velocities with a straight line \(G\), which for the time being is arbitrarily situated in space, \(\theta\) and \(\Theta\) angles of the plane passing through \(e\), \(c\), and the straight line \(G\), also arbitrarily placed through \(G\), and \(\theta'\) and \(\Theta'\) corresponding angles relating to the state after time \( t_2 - t_1\), we have the following equations:

\[ \frac{mv^2}2 + \frac{MV^2}2 = \frac{mc^2}2 + \frac{MC^2}2 \] \[mv\cos\phi' + MV\cos\Phi = mc\cos\phi + MV\cos\Phi \]

\[ mv\sin\varphi^\prime\sin\vartheta\ + MV\sin\Phi^\prime\cos\Theta^\prime = mc\sin\phi\cos\theta + MC\sin\Phi\cos\Theta\]
